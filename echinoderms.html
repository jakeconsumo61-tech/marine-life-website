Echinoderms

Sentinels of the seabed with skin of thorns. Lacking a brain but moving with hundreds of hydraulic feet, they mirror the stars of the sky on the floor of the abyss. Meet Echinodermata—sea stars and urchins, the radial wonders of the saltwater world.

Echinoderm

Banded Brittle Star

Unlike common starfish, Brittle Stars have long, slender arms that are sharply set off from a central disk. They move with a snake-like wriggling motion rather than creeping on tube feet.

Fun Fact

They are named "brittle" because they can intentionally shed an arm if grabbed by a predator. The arm wiggles to distract the enemy while the star escapes.

ScientificOphioderma appressum
HabitatTropical Reefs
SizeUp to 20 cm
DietDetritus & Small Organisms
Echinoderm

Red Sea Star

A classic five-armed starfish with a brilliant scarlet color. They are often found on rocky substrates where they use hundreds of tiny suction-cup feet to cling against strong currents.

Fun Fact

Sea stars don't have blood! Instead, they use a "water vascular system" to pump seawater through their bodies to move their feet and transport nutrients.

ScientificFromia milleporella
HabitatIndo-Pacific Reefs
SizeUp to 15 cm
DietBiofilm & Sponges
Echinoderm

Basket Star

The most complex-looking of the echinoderms, Basket Stars have arms that branch repeatedly into thousands of tiny tendrils, forming a living "basket" to trap plankton.

Fun Fact

During the day, they curl up into a tight, protective ball. At night, they unfurl their massive web of arms into the current to feed.

ScientificGorgonocephalus spp.
HabitatDeep or Cold Waters
SizeUp to 1 meter (Open)
DietZooplankton
Echinoderm

Pencil Urchin

Unlike the "pin-cushion" urchins, the Pencil Urchin has thick, blunt spines that look like antique slate pencils. These spines are often covered in tiny algae or hitchhiking organisms.

Fun Fact

They use their sturdy spines like stilts to walk across the reef and even to wedge themselves into rocky crevices so tightly that predators can't pull them out.

ScientificEucidaris tribuloides
HabitatAtlantic & Caribbean
SizeUp to 15 cm
DietAlgae & Seagrass
Echinoderm

Spotted Starfish

Featuring a tan or cream body with contrasting dark brown or blue spots, these stars are common in shallow sandy lagoons and sea grass beds.

Fun Fact

They are sometimes called "Marble Stars" due to their smooth, patterned texture which looks like polished stone under the water.

ScientificFromia monilis
HabitatWestern Pacific
SizeUp to 10 cm
DietDetritus & Microalgae
Echinoderm

Spiny Red Star

This starfish is covered in small, hard, white-tipped spines over a deep red or orange body. It is a common inhabitant of the Mediterranean and Atlantic coasts.

Fun Fact

The spines aren't just for defense; they are part of the skeleton and help the star maintain its shape in high-energy wave zones.

ScientificMarthasterias glacialis
HabitatMediterranean & Atlantic
SizeUp to 30 cm
DietMollusks & Fish
Echinoderm

Pink Sea Star

A large, soft-bodied starfish found in the Northeast Pacific. They are typically a pale pink or lavender color and are known for being quite heavy and robust.

Fun Fact

They are top-tier predators! They hunt by pushing their stomach out of their mouth and into the shells of clams to digest the prey alive.

ScientificPisaster brevispinus
HabitatPacific North America
SizeUp to 60 cm
DietClams & Sand Dollars
Echinoderm

Crown-of-Thorns

A massive, multi-armed starfish covered in long, venomous spines. They are notorious for eating coral polyps and can devastate entire reef systems if their population spikes.

Fun Fact

Their spines contain a neurotoxin that causes intense pain in humans. Ironically, their only major predator is the Giant Triton snail.

ScientificAcanthaster planci
HabitatIndo-Pacific Reefs
SizeUp to 40 cm
DietCoral Polyps
Echinoderm

Red Sea Urchin

The largest of the sea urchins, found in the shallow kelp forests of the North Pacific. They range from red to dark purple and play a vital role in kelp health.

Fun Fact

They are among the longest-living animals on Earth; some individuals are estimated to be over 200 years old, showing few signs of aging.

ScientificMesocentrotus franciscanus
HabitatNorth Pacific Kelp Forests
SizeUp to 19 cm (Body)
DietKelp & Seaweed
Echinoderm

Purple Sea Urchin

A common inhabitant of the tide pools along the West Coast of North America. Their vibrant purple spines are short and dense, giving them a fuzzy appearance.

Fun Fact

They have a specialized chewing organ called "Aristotle’s Lantern," which consists of five self-sharpening teeth to scrape algae off rocks.

ScientificStrongylocentrotus purpuratus
HabitatRocky Intertidal Zones
SizeUp to 10 cm
DietAlgae & Kelp
Echinoderm

Blue Sea Star

One of the most photographed creatures on the reef, this starfish is famous for its shocking, solid blue color. They have thick, round arms and a firm texture.

Fun Fact

If you see a Blue Star with arms of different lengths, it is likely regenerating limbs lost to a predator—a process that can take several months.

ScientificLinckia laevigata
HabitatTropical Indo-Pacific
SizeUp to 30 cm
DietDetritus & Algae
Echinoderm

Common Sea Star

The standard orange or purple star found along North Atlantic coasts. They are incredibly hardy and can survive in very cold, turbulent waters.

Fun Fact

They can smell their prey! They use sensory receptors at the tip of each arm to "taste" the water and locate mussel beds from a distance.

ScientificAsterias rubens
HabitatNorth Atlantic
SizeUp to 30 cm
DietMussels & Barnacles
Echinoderm

Juvenile Sea Star

Sea star larvae are microscopic and float in the plankton. When they "settle," they undergo a radical transformation into a tiny, symmetrical juvenile.

Fun Fact

A juvenile sea star the size of a grain of rice is already a fully functional hunter, using its tiny tube feet to find even smaller prey.

ScientificAsteroidea (Juvenile)
HabitatReef Crevices
Size1 mm to 1 cm
DietMicro-detritus
Marine Fish

Red Sea Bream

A highly prized fish in many cultures, the Red Sea Bream is known for its beautiful pinkish-red hue and its importance in the coastal ecosystems of Japan and Europe.

Fun Fact

In Japan, this fish is known as "Tai" and is a symbol of good luck and celebration, often served at weddings.

ScientificPagrus major
HabitatNorthwest Pacific
SizeUp to 1.2 meters
DietCrabs & Small Fish
Marine Fish

Ribbon Eel (Yellow)

This is the final life stage of the Ribbon Eel. In the yellow phase, the eel has reached full maturity and is a functional female, living in sand or rubble holes.

Fun Fact

They are protandrous hermaphrodites, meaning they all start as black juveniles, turn blue as males, and finally yellow as females!

ScientificRhinomuraena quaesita
HabitatIndo-Pacific
SizeUp to 1 meter
DietSmall Fish
Marine Fish

Nassau Grouper

A social and curious fish, the Nassau Grouper is a top predator of Caribbean reefs. They have a distinctive "tuning fork" pattern on their forehead.

Fun Fact

They are known to be very "friendly" to divers and often follow them around the reef like underwater puppies.

ScientificEpinephelus striatus
HabitatCaribbean & Atlantic
SizeUp to 1 meter
DietCrabs & Spiny Lobsters
Marine Fish

Clownfish

Also known as Anemonefish, these bright orange fish are famous for their symbiotic relationship with stinging sea anemones. They are one of the few species capable of living safely among the anemone's venomous tentacles.

Fun Fact

They aren't born immune to the sting! They perform a special "dance" to coat their bodies in the anemone's mucus, tricking the anemone into thinking the fish is part of itself.

ScientificAmphiprioninae
HabitatIndo-Pacific Reefs
Size7 to 10 cm
DietAlgae & Zooplankton
Marine Fish

Blue Tang

Famous for its vibrant royal blue body and yellow tail, the Blue Tang is a critical member of the coral reef. They are tireless grazers that help keep coral heads clean from overgrowing algae.

Fun Fact

They are "surgeons" of the sea! They have a small, retractable scalpel-like spine on each side of their tail base for defense against predators.

ScientificParacanthurus hepatus
HabitatIndo-Pacific Reefs
SizeUp to 30 cm
DietAlgae & Plankton
Freshwater Fish

Corydoras Catfish

Often called "Corys," these small, armored catfish are the vacuum cleaners of South American rivers. They are social bottom-dwellers that use their sensitive barbels to find food in the substrate.

Fun Fact

They are "intestinal breathers"—they can dash to the surface, gulp air, and absorb the oxygen through their gut to survive in low-oxygen waters.

ScientificCorydoras spp.
HabitatAmazon River Basin
Size2 to 10 cm
DietBenthic Detritus & Insects
Marine Fish

Blue Ring Angelfish

Named for the sapphire-blue ring located just behind its head, this majestic fish is a staple of Indo-Pacific coral gardens. They are known for their graceful swimming and striking facial markings.

Fun Fact

They are surprisingly vocal! When they feel threatened or territorial, they can produce loud, audible "drumming" sounds to scare away intruders.

ScientificPomacanthus annuarlis
HabitatTropical West Pacific
SizeUp to 45 cm
DietSponges & Tunicates
Marine Fish

Black Sea Bass

A robust, bottom-dwelling fish commonly found around shipwrecks and jetties. They are highly sought after by anglers for their fighting spirit and mottled charcoal-to-black coloration.

Fun Fact

They are protogynous hermaphrodites, meaning most start life as females and later transform into dominant males once they reach a certain age or size.

ScientificCentropristis striata
HabitatWestern Atlantic Coast
SizeUp to 60 cm
DietCrabs, Mussels & Small Fish
