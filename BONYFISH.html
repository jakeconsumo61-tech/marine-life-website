Cartilaginous Fish

Predators carved from gristle and grace. With skeletons of flexible cartilage and skin like sandpaper, they have patrolled the oceans since before the dinosaurs. Meet the Chondrichthyes—sharks, rays, and chimeras powered by sixth senses and ancient instincts.

Elasmobranch

Great White Shark

The ocean's most iconic apex predator, the Great White is known for its massive size and powerful serrated teeth. They are warm-blooded, allowing them to hunt in both temperate and cool waters across the globe.

Fun Fact

Great Whites don't just swim; they breach! They can launch their entire multi-ton bodies out of the water while hunting seals.

ScientificCarcharodon carcharias
HabitatGlobal Temperate Coasts
SizeUp to 6 meters
DietSeals, Sea Lions, & Fish
Elasmobranch

Tiger Shark

Named for the dark stripes on its sides that fade with age, the Tiger Shark is often called the "wastebasket of the sea" because of its incredibly varied diet and undiscriminating appetite.

Fun Fact

Their teeth are highly specialized with sideways-pointing tips and serrations strong enough to saw through the shells of large sea turtles.

ScientificGaleocerdo cuvier
HabitatTropical & Subtropical
SizeUp to 5 meters
DietTurtles, Birds, & Rays
Elasmobranch

Bull Shark

Bull Sharks are unique among large sharks for their ability to thrive in both saltwater and freshwater. They are stocky, powerful, and known for their unpredictable behavior in shallow coastal waters.

Fun Fact

They have been found thousands of miles upriver in the Amazon and Mississippi rivers, thanks to specialized kidneys that help them retain salt.

ScientificCarcharhinus leucas
HabitatEstuaries & Rivers
SizeUp to 3.5 meters
DietFish, Sharks, & Turtles
Elasmobranch

Grey Reef Shark

Commonly found near coral drop-offs, these sharks are social and often seen in large groups. They are sleek, fast, and play a vital role in maintaining the health of reef ecosystems.

Fun Fact

When threatened, they perform a "threat display" by hunching their back, dropping their pectoral fins, and swimming in a side-to-side exaggerated way.

ScientificCarcharhinus amblyrhynchos
HabitatIndo-Pacific Reefs
SizeUp to 1.9 meters
DietReef Fish & Octopus
Elasmobranch

Hammerhead Shark

The most recognizable shark in the world due to its T-shaped head, called a cephalofoil. This shape provides superior 360-degree vision and acts as a hydrodynamic wing for sharp turning.

Fun Fact

The cephalofoil is packed with sensory organs that allow them to detect the faint electrical pulses of stingrays buried deep in the sand.

ScientificSphyrna spp.
HabitatWorldwide Coastal
Size1 to 6 meters
DietFish, Rays, & Squid
Elasmobranch

Sand Tiger Shark

Despite their fierce appearance and jagged, protruding teeth, Sand Tigers are generally docile. They are often found hovering motionlessly near the seafloor or inside underwater caves.

Fun Fact

It is the only shark known to gulp air from the surface and store it in its stomach to maintain neutral buoyancy in the water.

ScientificCarcharias taurus
HabitatGlobal Subtropical
SizeUp to 3.2 meters
DietSmall Fish & Lobsters
Elasmobranch

Nurse Shark

Nurse sharks are sluggish bottom-dwellers with small mouths and two fleshy barbels near their nostrils. They use a "suction" method to pull prey out of crevices at night.

Fun Fact

Unlike most sharks, they don't need to swim to breathe. They use a buccal pumping method to manually pump water over their gills while resting.

ScientificGinglymostoma cirratum
HabitatAtlantic & East Pacific
SizeUp to 3 meters
DietShellfish & Sea Urchins
Elasmobranch

Whale Shark

The largest fish in the sea, the Whale Shark is a gentle giant. Despite its enormous size, it is a filter feeder, sifting microscopic plankton from massive amounts of water.

Fun Fact

The pattern of white spots on their skin is unique to every individual, much like a human fingerprint, allowing researchers to track them.

ScientificRhincodon typus
HabitatTropical Oceans
SizeUp to 12+ meters
DietPlankton & Krill
Elasmobranch

Zebra Shark

Zebra sharks have long tails that can be as long as their entire bodies. As adults, they are covered in spots, but they get their name from the striped patterns they possess as juveniles.

Fun Fact

They are capable of asexual reproduction (parthenogenesis), where a female can produce offspring without any contribution from a male.

ScientificStegostoma tigrinum
HabitatIndo-West Pacific
SizeUp to 2.5 meters
DietMollusks & Crabs
Elasmobranch

Spotted Eagle Ray

These stunning rays are known for their dark backs covered in white spots and their long, whip-like tails. They are active swimmers and are often seen leaping completely out of the water.

Fun Fact

They use their duck-bill shaped snouts to dig into the sandy seafloor to find buried clams and snails, which they crush with plate-like teeth.

ScientificAetobatus narinari
HabitatGlobal Tropical
SizeUp to 3m (wingspan)
DietCrabs, Clams, & Oysters
Elasmobranch

Southern Stingray

A diamond-shaped ray that spends most of its time buried in the sand with only its eyes and spiracles visible. They are a common sight for snorkelers in shallow Caribbean waters.

Fun Fact

They are famous at "Stingray City" in the Cayman Islands, where they have learned to associate the sound of boat engines with a free meal.

ScientificHypanus americanus
HabitatWestern Atlantic
SizeUp to 1.5m (width)
DietShrimp & Small Fish
Elasmobranch

Roughtail Stingray

One of the largest stingrays, the Roughtail is named for the thorny tubercles and jagged scales that line its tail. They prefer deeper shelf waters compared to their southern cousins.

Fun Fact

Their tail can be nearly double their body length, and they use those rough thorns as a physical deterrent against hungry sharks.

ScientificBathytoshia centroura
HabitatAtlantic Ocean
SizeUp to 2.6m (width)
DietBottom-dwelling Fish
Elasmobranch

Cownose Ray

Named for their unique notched snout that looks like a cow’s nose, these rays are highly migratory and are often seen swimming in massive schools numbering in the thousands.

Fun Fact

They are "synchronous" swimmers; in their massive schools, they all flap their wings in unison, creating a beautiful golden wave in the sea.

ScientificRhinoptera bonasus
HabitatWestern Atlantic Coast
SizeUp to 1m (wingspan)
DietBivalves & Crustaceans
Elasmobranch

Manta Ray

The largest of all rays, Mantas are graceful filter feeders. They have large "cephalic fins" on their heads that they use like funnels to push plankton-rich water into their mouths.

Fun Fact

Mantas have the largest brain-to-body ratio of any cold-blooded fish and may even be able to recognize themselves in mirrors.

ScientificMobula spp.
HabitatOpen Tropical Oceans
SizeUp to 7m (wingspan)
DietZooplankton
Elasmobranch

Common Stingray

Members of the Dasyatis and Hypanus genera, these rays are masters of camouflaging with the sandy bottom. Their venomous tail spine is used strictly for self-defense against predators.

Fun Fact

If they lose their stinger, it’s not the end! Stingrays naturally shed and regrow their venomous barbs throughout their lives.

ScientificDasyatis/Hypanus spp.
HabitatCoastal Shelves
SizeVaries (60cm - 2m)
DietWorms & Mollusks
Elasmobranch

Guitarfish

Also known as Shovelnose Rays, these fish look like a cross between a shark and a ray. They have the flattened front body of a ray but the muscular tail and swimming style of a shark.

Fun Fact

Despite their "shovelnose" appearance, they don't use their heads to dig; they use them as hydrofoils to stay pinned to the seafloor in strong currents.

ScientificRhinobatos spp.
HabitatWarm Coastal Waters
SizeUp to 1.5 meters
DietCrustaceans & Small Fish
Anthozoan

Hammer Coral

This "Large Polyp Stony" (LPS) coral is famous for its tentacles that end in T-shaped or anchor-shaped tips. They are highly aggressive toward neighbors, using "sweeper" tentacles to sting anyone who grows too close.

Fun Fact

During the day, the polyps expand so much that they completely hide their hard calcium carbonate skeleton underneath.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific
Size Colonies up to 1 m
Diet Zooplankton & Light
Anthozoan

Bubble Coral

Bubble corals have distinctive water-filled "grape" vesicles that expand during the day to maximize surface area for light absorption. At night, the bubbles retract to reveal stinging feeding tentacles.

Fun Fact

The "bubbles" are actually modified tentacles. They are very delicate and can easily pop if touched by a predator (or a clumsy diver!).

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Red Sea & Indo-Pacific
Size Up to 1 m colonies
Diet Plankton & Light
Anthozoan

Magnificent Anemone

Known for its vibrant column colors (often purple, red, or blue), this is one of the largest anemone species. It is a primary host for many species of clownfish, offering them protection within its long, blunt-tipped tentacles.

Fun Fact

When stressed or when the tide goes out, this anemone can pull itself into a tight ball, looking like a colorful underwater cabbage or pumpkin.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific
Size Up to 1 m diameter
Diet Fish, Shrimp & Light
Anthozoan

Devil's Hand

This soft coral is named for its finger-like projections that extend from a thick, leathery base. Unlike stony corals, it lacks a hard skeleton, instead using tiny internal spikes called sclerites to maintain its unique, hand-like shape.

Fun Fact

This coral is a chemical warrior! It produces toxic compounds called terpenes to prevent algae from growing on it and to stop other corals from crowding its space.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific Reefs
Size Up to 30 cm
Diet Light & Micro-plankton
Anthozoan

Honeycomb Coral

Honeycomb coral forms massive, boulder-like colonies with a surface covered in perfectly geometric, crater-like polyps. This rigid pattern gives it the appearance of a stone beehive and makes it one of the most easily recognizable hard corals.

Fun Fact

Because their skeletons are so dense and sturdy, Honeycomb corals often serve as the "bedrock" of the reef, surviving major storms that break apart more delicate branching corals.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific Reefs
Size Colonies up to 5 m
Diet Light & Zooplankton
