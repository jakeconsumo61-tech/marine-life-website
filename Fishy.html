Cnidarian

Jellyfish that never die. Corals that build rainforests of the sea. Anemones that glow in the dark. Meet Cnidaria—11,000 species armed with microscopic harpoons and 700 million years of secrets.

Cnidarian

Purple-Striped Jelly

Purple-striped jellies are striking invertebrates known for their vivid, "lava lamp" patterns and long, trailing tentacles. They pulse gracefully through coastal waters, using their stinging cells to capture prey and ward off potential predators.

Fun Fact

Young Cancer crabs are often found living inside or on the bell of these jellyfish, hitching a ride while eating parasitic organisms that might harm the jelly.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat California & Mexico
Size Up to 100 cm
Diet Zooplankton & small jellies

Spotted Jelly

Also known as the Lagoon Jelly, these translucent beauties are covered in distinct white spots. Unlike most jellies, they are "farmers"—they host tiny algae in their tissues that provide them with energy from sunlight as they follow the sun across the water.

Fun Fact

Because they rely on photosynthesis, Spotted Jellies perform a daily migration, swimming toward the sun during the day and descending into deeper, nutrient-rich waters at night.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat South Pacific & Indian Ocean
Size Up to 15 cm
Diet Zooplankton & Sun-energy (Algae)
Cnidarian

Moon Jellyfish

Moon jellies are the ethereal "ghosts" of the ocean. Translucent and circular, they drift primarily at the surface. Instead of long tentacles, they use a fine fringe of cilia to sweep microscopic food toward their mouths.

Fun Fact

Moon jellies are space travelers! In 1991, over 2,000 moon jelly polyps were sent into orbit on the Space Shuttle Columbia to study how weightlessness affects their development.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Global Coastal Waters
Size 25 - 40 cm
Diet Plankton & Mollusks

Compass Jellyfish

A striking summer visitor to coastal waters, the Compass Jelly is named for the distinctive brown V-shaped markings on its bell. They are active hunters with a painful sting used to subdue small fish and other jellies.

Fun Fact

This species is protandrous, meaning they start their lives as males and later transform into females as they grow larger and more mature.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Northeast Atlantic
Size Up to 30 cm
Diet Small fish & invertebrates
Cnidarian

Mediterranean Jelly

Often called the "Fried Egg Jelly" due to the yellow dome in the center of its white bell, this species features beautiful purple, button-like appendages on its mouth-arms. They are one of the most common jellies in the Mediterranean.

Fun Fact

Small fish, like juvenile horse mackerel, often hide inside the jelly's frilly mouth-arms for protection, as the jelly's sting is too weak to hurt them (or humans!).

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Mediterranean Sea
Size Up to 35 cm
Diet Zooplankton & Algae-energy
Ctenophore

Comb Jelly

Comb jellies are oval-shaped marvels that move using eight rows of tiny hair-like cilia called "combs." They do not have stinging cells; instead, most use sticky cells to snag prey as they shimmer through the water.

Fun Fact

They aren't actually glowing! The rainbow colors seen on their combs are caused by light diffracting (scattering) off the moving hairs, like a living prism.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Global Oceans
Size 1 cm to 1.5 m (varies)
Diet Small crustaceans & Larvae
Cnidarian

Cannonball Jelly

Looking like a smooth, round dome, the Cannonball Jelly is a powerful swimmer. It has a thick, muscular bell and short, chunky arms used for propulsion and capturing food in the water column.

Fun Fact

When threatened, they secrete a toxic mucus that can drive away predators and even stun small fish in the immediate area.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat North & South America
Size Up to 25 cm
Diet Zooplankton & Red Drum larvae
Cnidarian

Mauve Stinger

A small but formidable jelly, the Mauve Stinger is known for its beautiful purple coloration and its bioluminescence—it glows in the dark when disturbed by waves or passing ships.

Fun Fact

Unlike most jellies that stay near the coast to breed, the Mauve Stinger lives its entire life cycle in the open ocean (holoplanktonic).

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Warm & Temperate Waters
Size Up to 10 cm
Diet Eggs, Larvae, & other Jellies
Cnidarian

Lion's Mane Jelly

The largest known species of jellyfish in the world. Its massive bell is complemented by a "mane" of thousands of hair-like tentacles that can stretch longer than a blue whale.

Fun Fact

The largest recorded specimen had tentacles reaching 37 meters (120 ft) long—that's longer than three school buses parked end-to-end!

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Arctic & North Pacific
Size Up to 2 m (bell diameter)
Diet Fish, Crustaceans, & Jellies
Cnidarian

Pacific Sea Nettle

With its distinctive golden-brown bell and long, spiraling white mouth-arms, the Pacific Sea Nettle is a masterpiece of ocean movement. Their sting is potent enough to paralyze small prey instantly.

Fun Fact

They are "passive" hunters; they don't chase prey, but rather drift with their tentacles spread out like a giant web to catch whatever floats by.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat East Pacific Coast
Size Up to 50 cm
Diet Zooplankton & small fish
Hydrozoan

Hydrozoan Jelly

Hydrozoans are often tiny and transparent. Unlike "true" jellies, many are actually colonies of specialized individuals living together, like the famous (and dangerous) Portuguese Man o' War.

Fun Fact

Some hydrozoans, like the "Immortal Jellyfish," can revert their cells back to a younger state, effectively starting their life cycle over again forever.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Worldwide (Salt & Fresh)
Size 1 mm to 30 cm
Diet Tiny plankton & Eggs

Blue Blubber Jelly

A favorite in coastal Australia, these jellies have a distinctive creamy white or sky-blue color. They lack a single central mouth, instead possessing many small mouth openings along their textured arms.

Fun Fact

Their color isn't always blue! Depending on the algae living in their tissues and the minerals in the water, they can appear white, brown, or even deep purple.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific (Australia)
Size Up to 35 cm
Diet Plankton & small detritus
Hydrozoan

Flower Hat Jelly

The Flower Hat Jelly is a rare and striking species with a pinstriped bell and a "hat" of multi-colored, neon-tipped tentacles. Unlike most jellies, it spent much of its time resting on the seafloor, looking like a vibrant, underwater flower.

Fun Fact

When not in use, their tentacles coil up tightly against the rim of their bell, giving them their unique "flower hat" appearance.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Japan, Brazil & Argentina
Size Up to 15 cm
Diet Small fish & invertebrates
Hydrozoan

Crystal Jelly

Completely transparent and nearly invisible in the water, the Crystal Jelly is a marvel of biological engineering. It possesses over 100 tiny tentacles and is famous for its ability to produce brilliant flashes of green bioluminescent light.

Fun Fact

This jelly changed modern science; it is the source of GFP (Green Fluorescent Protein), which allows researchers to watch biological processes happen inside living cells.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat North American West Coast
Size Up to 25 cm
Diet Copepods & other Jellies
Anthozoan

Bubble Tip Anemone

The Bubble Tip Anemone is a favorite among divers for its bulbous, balloon-like tentacle tips. They provide a safe, stinging haven for clownfish, creating one of the ocean's most iconic symbiotic partnerships within the coral reef ecosystem.

Fun Fact

These anemones are "shifters"—if they aren't happy with the light or water flow, they can detach their foot and slowly crawl across the reef to find a brand-new home!

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Tropical Coral Reefs
Size Up to 30 cm
Diet Plankton & Small Fish
Anthozoan

Brain Coral

Named for its uncanny resemblance to a human brain, this hard coral grows in massive hemispherical shapes. The "maze" patterns are actually rows of individual polyps that share a common skeleton.

Fun Fact

Brain corals are incredibly slow growers, but they are the giants of the reef—some colonies can live for up to 900 years!

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Tropical Reefs
Size Up to 1.8 m
Diet Plankton & Light
Anthozoan

Hammer Coral

This "Large Polyp Stony" (LPS) coral is famous for its tentacles that end in T-shaped or anchor-shaped tips. They are highly aggressive toward neighbors, using "sweeper" tentacles to sting anyone who grows too close.

Fun Fact

During the day, the polyps expand so much that they completely hide their hard calcium carbonate skeleton underneath.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific
Size Colonies up to 1 m
Diet Zooplankton & Light
Anthozoan

Bubble Coral

Bubble corals have distinctive water-filled "grape" vesicles that expand during the day to maximize surface area for light absorption. At night, the bubbles retract to reveal stinging feeding tentacles.

Fun Fact

The "bubbles" are actually modified tentacles. They are very delicate and can easily pop if touched by a predator (or a clumsy diver!).

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Red Sea & Indo-Pacific
Size Up to 1 m colonies
Diet Plankton & Light
Anthozoan

Magnificent Anemone

Known for its vibrant column colors (often purple, red, or blue), this is one of the largest anemone species. It is a primary host for many species of clownfish, offering them protection within its long, blunt-tipped tentacles.

Fun Fact

When stressed or when the tide goes out, this anemone can pull itself into a tight ball, looking like a colorful underwater cabbage or pumpkin.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific
Size Up to 1 m diameter
Diet Fish, Shrimp & Light
Anthozoan

Devil's Hand

This soft coral is named for its finger-like projections that extend from a thick, leathery base. Unlike stony corals, it lacks a hard skeleton, instead using tiny internal spikes called sclerites to maintain its unique, hand-like shape.

Fun Fact

This coral is a chemical warrior! It produces toxic compounds called terpenes to prevent algae from growing on it and to stop other corals from crowding its space.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific Reefs
Size Up to 30 cm
Diet Light & Micro-plankton
Anthozoan

Honeycomb Coral

Honeycomb coral forms massive, boulder-like colonies with a surface covered in perfectly geometric, crater-like polyps. This rigid pattern gives it the appearance of a stone beehive and makes it one of the most easily recognizable hard corals.

Fun Fact

Because their skeletons are so dense and sturdy, Honeycomb corals often serve as the "bedrock" of the reef, surviving major storms that break apart more delicate branching corals.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific Reefs
Size Colonies up to 5 m
Diet Light & Zooplankton
