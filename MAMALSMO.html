Marine Mammals

Warm-blooded giants in a cold-water world. With songs that travel oceans and spirits that crave the air, they represent the ultimate return to the sea. Meet the Marine Mammals—whales, dolphins, and seals, where high intelligence meets the vast blue frontier.

Mammal

Humpback Whale

Famous for their acrobatic "breaching" and complex songs, Humpbacks are migratory giants. They travel thousands of miles between cold feeding grounds and warm breeding lagoons.

Fun Fact

They use a technique called "bubble-net feeding," where a group blows bubbles in a circle to trap fish like a giant net.

ScientificMegaptera novaeangliae
HabitatGlobal Oceans
SizeUp to 16 meters
DietKrill & Small Fish
Mammal

Bottlenose Dolphin

Highly intelligent and social, these dolphins are known for their playful behavior and curved mouths that give them a permanent "smile." They live in tight-knit groups called pods.

Fun Fact

Dolphins have individual "signature whistles" that act like names, allowing pod members to call out to specific friends.

ScientificTursiops truncatus
HabitatWorldwide Warm Waters
Size2 to 4 meters
DietFish & Squid
Mammal

Beluga Whale

Known as the "canaries of the sea" due to their high-pitched chirps, Belugas are easily recognized by their pure white color and protuberant forehead (the melon).

Fun Fact

Unlike most whales, Belugas have flexible neck vertebrae, allowing them to turn their heads up, down, and side-to-side.

ScientificDelphinapterus leucas
HabitatArctic & Sub-Arctic
Size3 to 5 meters
DietFish & Crustaceans
Mammal

Harbor Seal

"True" seals, like the Harbor Seal, lack external ear flaps and crawl on their bellies while on land. They are sleek, torpedo-shaped divers that thrive in temperate coastal waters.

Fun Fact

When sleeping underwater, seals can close their nostrils voluntarily and stay submerged for up to 30 minutes at a time.

ScientificPhoca vitulina
HabitatNorth Atlantic & Pacific
SizeUp to 1.8 meters
DietFish, Crustaceans & Squid
Mammal

Walrus

Iconic for their long tusks and stiff whiskers, Walruses are massive Arctic mammals that use sea ice as platforms for resting and giving birth between foraging trips.

Fun Fact

Their whiskers (vibrissae) are incredibly sensitive, allowing them to detect tiny shellfish in the dark, muddy ocean floor.

ScientificOdobenus rosmarus
HabitatArctic Circle
SizeUp to 3.5 meters
DietClams & Mollusks
Mammal

California Sea Lion

Unlike true seals, Sea Lions have external ear flaps and can "walk" on land using their large flippers. They are loud, fast, and extremely agile both in and out of the water.

Fun Fact

They are the fastest of all pinnipeds (seals/walruses), reaching swimming speeds of up to 25 miles per hour.

ScientificZalophus californianus
HabitatEastern North Pacific
SizeUp to 2.4 meters
DietFish & Squid
Mammal

Elephant Seal

Named for the large, trunk-like noses of the adult males, these are the giants of the seal world. They are extraordinary divers, descending deep into the ocean to hunt.

Fun Fact

Southern Elephant Seals can dive to depths of over 2,000 meters and stay underwater for up to two hours!

ScientificMirounga spp.
HabitatPacific & Antarctic
SizeUp to 6 meters (Males)
DietSquid & Sharks
Mammal

Manatee

Often called "sea cows," Manatees are slow-moving herbivores. They spend most of their time grazing on seagrass in shallow rivers and coastal estuaries.

Fun Fact

Their closest living land relative is actually the elephant, sharing similar skin and specialized "marching" molars.

ScientificTrichechus spp.
HabitatTropical Coastal Waters
Size3 to 4 meters
DietSeagrass & Algae
Mammal

Sea Otter

The smallest of the marine mammals, Sea Otters lack blubber. Instead, they rely on the densest fur in the animal kingdom to stay warm in the chilly Pacific waters.

Fun Fact

They are tool users; they will often place a rock on their chest and use it to crack open hard shells while floating.

ScientificEnhydra lutris
HabitatNorth Pacific Coasts
Size1 to 1.5 meters
DietSea Urchins & Crabs
Mammal

Sperm Whale

The largest of the toothed whales, Sperm Whales are known for their massive block-shaped heads containing "spermaceti," a waxy substance used for buoyancy and echolocation.

Fun Fact

They have the largest brain of any creature to ever live on Earth—six times the size of a human brain!

ScientificPhyseter macrocephalus
HabitatGlobal Deep Oceans
SizeUp to 18 meters
DietGiant Squid & Fish
Mammal

Bearded Seal

This Arctic seal is named for its thick, bushy mustache of vibrant whiskers. They are benthic feeders, meaning they find their food along the bottom of the icy sea.

Fun Fact

The whiskers are actually smooth when they are wet but curl into tight, elegant spirals as they dry out.

ScientificErignathus barbatus
HabitatArctic & North Pacific
Size2.1 to 2.7 meters
DietClams, Fish & Shrimp
Elasmobranch

Roughtail Stingray

One of the largest stingrays, the Roughtail is named for the thorny tubercles and jagged scales that line its tail. They prefer deeper shelf waters compared to their southern cousins.

Fun Fact

Their tail can be nearly double their body length, and they use those rough thorns as a physical deterrent against hungry sharks.

ScientificBathytoshia centroura
HabitatAtlantic Ocean
SizeUp to 2.6m (width)
DietBottom-dwelling Fish
Elasmobranch

Cownose Ray

Named for their unique notched snout that looks like a cow’s nose, these rays are highly migratory and are often seen swimming in massive schools numbering in the thousands.

Fun Fact

They are "synchronous" swimmers; in their massive schools, they all flap their wings in unison, creating a beautiful golden wave in the sea.

ScientificRhinoptera bonasus
HabitatWestern Atlantic Coast
SizeUp to 1m (wingspan)
DietBivalves & Crustaceans
Elasmobranch

Manta Ray

The largest of all rays, Mantas are graceful filter feeders. They have large "cephalic fins" on their heads that they use like funnels to push plankton-rich water into their mouths.

Fun Fact

Mantas have the largest brain-to-body ratio of any cold-blooded fish and may even be able to recognize themselves in mirrors.

ScientificMobula spp.
HabitatOpen Tropical Oceans
SizeUp to 7m (wingspan)
DietZooplankton
Elasmobranch

Common Stingray

Members of the Dasyatis and Hypanus genera, these rays are masters of camouflaging with the sandy bottom. Their venomous tail spine is used strictly for self-defense against predators.

Fun Fact

If they lose their stinger, it’s not the end! Stingrays naturally shed and regrow their venomous barbs throughout their lives.

ScientificDasyatis/Hypanus spp.
HabitatCoastal Shelves
SizeVaries (60cm - 2m)
DietWorms & Mollusks
Elasmobranch

Guitarfish

Also known as Shovelnose Rays, these fish look like a cross between a shark and a ray. They have the flattened front body of a ray but the muscular tail and swimming style of a shark.

Fun Fact

Despite their "shovelnose" appearance, they don't use their heads to dig; they use them as hydrofoils to stay pinned to the seafloor in strong currents.

ScientificRhinobatos spp.
HabitatWarm Coastal Waters
SizeUp to 1.5 meters
DietCrustaceans & Small Fish
Anthozoan

Hammer Coral

This "Large Polyp Stony" (LPS) coral is famous for its tentacles that end in T-shaped or anchor-shaped tips. They are highly aggressive toward neighbors, using "sweeper" tentacles to sting anyone who grows too close.

Fun Fact

During the day, the polyps expand so much that they completely hide their hard calcium carbonate skeleton underneath.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific
Size Colonies up to 1 m
Diet Zooplankton & Light
Anthozoan

Bubble Coral

Bubble corals have distinctive water-filled "grape" vesicles that expand during the day to maximize surface area for light absorption. At night, the bubbles retract to reveal stinging feeding tentacles.

Fun Fact

The "bubbles" are actually modified tentacles. They are very delicate and can easily pop if touched by a predator (or a clumsy diver!).

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Red Sea & Indo-Pacific
Size Up to 1 m colonies
Diet Plankton & Light
Anthozoan

Magnificent Anemone

Known for its vibrant column colors (often purple, red, or blue), this is one of the largest anemone species. It is a primary host for many species of clownfish, offering them protection within its long, blunt-tipped tentacles.

Fun Fact

When stressed or when the tide goes out, this anemone can pull itself into a tight ball, looking like a colorful underwater cabbage or pumpkin.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific
Size Up to 1 m diameter
Diet Fish, Shrimp & Light
Anthozoan

Devil's Hand

This soft coral is named for its finger-like projections that extend from a thick, leathery base. Unlike stony corals, it lacks a hard skeleton, instead using tiny internal spikes called sclerites to maintain its unique, hand-like shape.

Fun Fact

This coral is a chemical warrior! It produces toxic compounds called terpenes to prevent algae from growing on it and to stop other corals from crowding its space.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific Reefs
Size Up to 30 cm
Diet Light & Micro-plankton
Anthozoan

Honeycomb Coral

Honeycomb coral forms massive, boulder-like colonies with a surface covered in perfectly geometric, crater-like polyps. This rigid pattern gives it the appearance of a stone beehive and makes it one of the most easily recognizable hard corals.

Fun Fact

Because their skeletons are so dense and sturdy, Honeycomb corals often serve as the "bedrock" of the reef, surviving major storms that break apart more delicate branching corals.

Type Invertebrate
Habitat Indo-Pacific Reefs
Size Colonies up to 5 m
Diet Light & Zooplankton
